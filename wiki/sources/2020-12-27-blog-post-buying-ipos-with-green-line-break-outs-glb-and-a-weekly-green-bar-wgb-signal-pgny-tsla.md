# WW 2020-12-27 — Buying IPOs with GLB and WGB signal; $PGNY $TSLA

**Source:** [Blog post: Buying IPOs with Green Line Break-outs (GLB) and a Weekly Green Bar (WGB) signal; $PGNY $TSLA](../../raw/posts/2020-12-27-blog-post-buying-ipos-with-green-line-break-outs-glb-and-a-weekly-green-bar-wgb-signal-pgny-tsla.md) · [original](https://wishingwealthblog.com/2020/12/blog-post-buying-ipos-with-green-line-break-outs-glb-and-a-weekly-green-bar-wgb-signal-pgny-tsla/) · tier: teaching

## What it covers

A teaching post that walks through why IPOs are excellent GLB candidates, explains how the green line is drawn (monthly chart, ATH held ≥3 months), and introduces the WGB (weekly green bar) as the secondary entry signal when you miss the GLB. Uses Facebook as an historical example of the IPO GLB logic (May 2012 IPO at $42, fell 61%, had GLB 17 months later). Uses PGNY and TSLA as current worked examples. Provides the WGB definition and the trailing stop rule.

## Key claims

- **IPO GLB logic:** post-IPO frenzied buying drives the stock to a high; it then declines as insiders profit; the GLB occurs when the stock finally trades above that original peak at a new ATH — evidence of strong buying that absorbed all overhead supply. He uses Facebook (IPO May 2012, GLB September 2013, 17 months later, after -61% decline) as the canonical example.
- **TC2000 alert:** as soon as he draws the green line he sets a TC2000 alert valid for one year to notify him when the stock trades above the line.
- **Mental stop:** he uses a mental stop for GLB positions (not a hard stop), selling if the stock *closes* below the green line. This avoids being stopped out on intraday dips below the green line that recover by the close.
- **WGB as the add/second-chance signal:** a WGB occurs when a stock has traded below its rising 4wk average during the week but closed above it, the close is higher than the prior week, and 4wk>10wk>30wk. He uses PGNY as the example.
- **WGB trailing stop rule:** place stop at the low of the most recent WGB; raise to the low of each successive WGB; exit when the stock trades below that low. He marks 6 of 8 WGBs in TSLA's advance after a GLB where this strategy worked.
- He credits Bill O'Neil's reliance on weekly charts as the right timeframe for holding growth stocks.
