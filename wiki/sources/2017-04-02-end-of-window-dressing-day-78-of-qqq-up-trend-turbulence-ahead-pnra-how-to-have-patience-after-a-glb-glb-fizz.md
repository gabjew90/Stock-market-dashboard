# WW 2017-04-02 — Patience after a GLB: PNRA and FIZZ examples

**Source:** [End of window dressing; day 78 of $QQQ up-trend; turbulence ahead? $PNRA, how to have patience after a GLB; GLB: $FIZZ](../../raw/posts/2017-04-02-end-of-window-dressing-day-78-of-qqq-up-trend-turbulence-ahead-pnra-how-to-have-patience-after-a-glb-glb-fizz.md) · [original](https://wishingwealthblog.com/2017/04/end-of-window-dressing-day-78-of-qqq-up-trend-turbulence-ahead-pnra-how-to-have-patience-after-a-glb-glb-fizz/) · tier: trade_example

## What it covers

A worked post-GLB patience lesson using PNRA and FIZZ. PNRA broke out to an ATH (GLB) on above-average volume, then went *sideways for 23 days* — the post emphasizes how psychologically difficult patience is in real time vs. how easy it looks on a retrospective chart. The RWB pattern on the daily chart helped: PNRA never closed below all six red lines (RLC never = 0), which gave a hold signal throughout the consolidation. FIZZ is the contrast: it never even dipped into the white space between the red and blue lines — a perfect 12/12/6/6 pattern from before its GLB. Both eventually continued higher.

## Key claims

- Jesse Livermore: "it was his sitting tight not his trading that made him big money." GLBs often require extended periods of patience.
- After a GLB, stocks frequently consolidate for weeks before continuing. "In reality it often means that we buyers need to have incredible patience to hold on" — looking at a retrospective chart makes patience seem obvious; living through it is psychologically hard.
- The daily RWB pattern as a hold anchor: "PNRA remained in a nice RWB pattern after its GLB. It never closed below all of its red lines (RLC never equalled 0)." Even though the daily low dipped into the white space 4 times (shown as purple dots), the *close* always recovered back within the red lines.
- The mental-stop preference: "I prefer to use mental stops sometimes and to check my positions around 15 minutes before the close each day." A close below all 6 red lines (RLC = 0) is a caution signal, but he notes that "even a close below all 6 red lines is not necessarily the preferred exit point" — he might reduce the position or wait for a close below a blue line.
- After a GLB, the green line remains the hard exit trigger: "I must sell if the stock closes back below the green break-out line." PNRA never breached its green line.
- FIZZ example: a stock in a perfect daily RWB (12/12/6/6) that had no consolidation post-GLB — it just kept rising. Not all GLBs have 23-day consolidations.
- SPY/DIA RWB interpretation in April 2017: the white space between red and blue lines was diminishing — only a "minimal RWB pattern" — vs. QQQ at 12/12/6/6 showing stronger tech-driven momentum.
