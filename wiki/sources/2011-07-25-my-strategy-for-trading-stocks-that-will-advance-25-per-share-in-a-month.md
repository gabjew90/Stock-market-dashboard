# WW 2011-07-25 — My strategy for trading stocks that will advance $25 per share in a month

**Source:** [My strategy for trading stocks that will advance $25 per share in a month](../../raw/posts/2011-07-25-my-strategy-for-trading-stocks-that-will-advance-25-per-share-in-a-month.md) · [original](https://wishingwealthblog.com/2011/07/my-strategy-for-trading-stocks-that-will-advance-25-per-share-in-a-month/) · tier: teaching

## What it covers

A detailed post explaining why Dr. Wish focuses on high-priced stocks (>$80 and ideally >$100) and how he uses deep-in-the-money (DITM) call options to control these stocks at roughly 10–20% of their purchase price. The $25-in-a-month framing is his filter: stocks that advance the most dollars in 30 days are almost always priced above $80 at the start of the period (90% rule based on his TC2000 scan). He illustrates the DITM call mechanics with NFLX (then trading at $276.58) as a step-by-step example.

## Key claims

- He ran a TC2000 scan of all stocks that advanced $25 or more in the prior 30 days. Found 21 stocks; 90% (19/21) were priced above $80 thirty days prior; 17 were above $100.
- Dollar-move focus over percentage-move focus: a 20% move on a $10 stock is only $2; on a $100 stock it is $20. He finds it psychologically easier to hold through a $20 move than a $2 move.
- High-priced stocks are also less vulnerable to high-frequency day-traders (capital requirement), and their price is high for a reason — funds are bidding them up.
- DITM call mechanics: use a near-month call that expires 3–6 weeks out, costs less than 20% of the stock price, and has a strike deep enough in the money that the break-even is close to the current stock price. Example: NFLX at $276.58 → August 230 call at $49 per share → break-even $279 → profit as if owning 100 shares once above $279.
- With a DITM option you risk only the option premium (not the full stock cost), but gain dollar-for-dollar above the break-even, just like owning the shares.
- He would never hold to expiration if the stock broke support; he would sell the option at the best available price.
- The $4,900 option controlled $27,658 of NFLX — effectively 18% down payment. He states explicitly: do not use the leverage to buy *more* shares than you could have afforded; use it to reduce capital outlay for the same exposure.
