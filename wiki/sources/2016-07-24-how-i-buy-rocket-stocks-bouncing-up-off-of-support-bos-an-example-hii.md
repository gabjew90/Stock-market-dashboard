# WW 2016-07-24 — BOS mechanics: the Bollinger Band bounce and stop placement

**Source:** [How I buy rocket stocks bouncing up off of support (BOS): Examples: $HII and $AWK](../../raw/posts/2016-07-24-how-i-buy-rocket-stocks-bouncing-up-off-of-support-bos-an-example-hii.md) · [original](https://wishingwealthblog.com/2016/07/how-i-buy-rocket-stocks-bouncing-up-off-of-support-bos-an-example-hii/) · tier: teaching

## What it covers

A detailed explanation of the BOS (Bounce off Support) mechanics. The primary oversold indicator is revealed: the **lower 15.2 daily Bollinger Band** (a 15-day moving average with 2-standard-deviation bands). Dr. Wish scans for RWB stocks whose daily low has traded down to or below this band and are now bouncing up. He uses the concrete example of HII (Huntington Ingalls), which had bounced off its lower BB multiple times, including showing exceptional relative strength during a Grexit-related market decline. He places the stop just below the low of the bounce.

## Key claims

- The primary BOS signal is a bounce up from the **lower 15.2 daily Bollinger Band** (15-day average, 2 standard deviations).
- "My primary measure of oversold is a bounce up from the lower 15.2 daily Bollinger Band."
- Once a list of bouncing stocks is found, he investigates trading volume, fundamentals, market trend. But these "do not matter much" in the end because "it is impossible to reliably predict which stock's bounce will hold" — so the stop placement is more important than candidate selection.
- He places a buy order on strength (if the stock trades above the prior day's high on Monday) and sets the stop near the low of the bounce (e.g., 167.19 for HII).
- He runs this strategy successfully with "all types of stocks and ETFs."
- Many purchases will fail — this is accepted. "Each small loss brings me closer to the next big gain — no ego allowed."
- When a bounce holds, he can ride the stock and even add more to his position so that "relatively few large profits make up for many small losses."
- HII showed relative strength during a correction (Bottom 2 during Grexit was *higher* than Bottom 1 from an earlier correction), underscoring the value of finding strong stocks.
